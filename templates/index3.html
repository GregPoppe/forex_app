MACD

Moving Average Convergence/Divergence oscillator popularly called the MACD or "Mac-Dee" is considered the most effective momentum indicators available developed by Gerald Appel in the late 1970s. It is a special indicator because it brings together momentum and trend in one indicator. The MACD oscillates either above or below the zero line also called the centerline as the moving averages converge, cross, and diverge. Signals are generated when the signal line crossovers, centerline crossovers and divergences, this is particularly noteworthy for traders. MACD is about converging and diverging of two averages. When moving averages move towards each other is known as convergence. And the two moving averages move away from each other it is known as divergence. MACD movements are possibly by the shorter moving average (12 days). While on the other hand the 26 day is considered a longer moving average and is less reactive to price changes. When the 12-day exponential moving average (EMA) is above the 26 day EMA a positive MACD is observed. Thus, upside momentum is increasing. While if the 12 day EMA is below the 26 day EMA, a negative MACD is observed. This means the downside momentum is decreasing. A signal line also known as the trigger line is generated by taking a nine period moving average of the MACD.


RSI

J. Welles Wilder developed the RSI in 1978, which stands for the relative strength index. It measures the speed and change of recent price movements as a momentum oscillator usually in a 14-day period. The RSI fluctuates between zero and 100. This index is used to evaluate the certain conditions such as oversold and overbought. When the indictor is above 70, the signal is considered overbought and when it is below 30 it is oversold. The RSI measures the price change in relationship to the recent price of highs and lows. RSI is often combined with the MACD and creates a technical background. RSI that follows in the same direction of the trend signifies potential trend strength and increasing speed of the trend is known as convergence. On the other hand, divergence is RSI that moves in the opposite of the trend's direction over a period of time signifies potential trend weakness and/or decreasing trend momentum.


Williams Percent Range

Williams Percent Range or commonly known as % R developed by Larry Williams. The %R oscillates between 0 and -100. It shows how the current price compares to the highest price over a certain period in the past. Typically, the past is considered 14 periods. 14 periods on a weekly chart that is 14 weeks, while on an hourly chart 14 hours. When the indicator is near zero is shows the price is trading near or above the highest high during the past period. If indicator is near -100, the price is trading near or below the lowest low during the past period. Above -50 and the price is trading within the upper portion of the 14 period range; below -50 and the price is trading in the lower portion of the 14 period range. The most common use for the Williams %R is for "overbought" and "oversold" readings and momentum confirmations and failures. Just using %R for entries will produce a large number of false signals; false signals are when the indicator signals that the trend is turning, but in fact it doesn't. Only taking signals in the same direction as the trend can help in this regard. Using the indicator in conjunction with other indicators or price and trend analysis will help filter out some of the false signals.


Stochastic Oscillator

In the 1950s, George Lane developed Stochastic Oscillator (STOCH). It is an indicator that shows the location of the close relative to the high-low range over a set number of periods. It is similar to the Williams Percent Range. The indicator can range from 0 to 100. The Stochastics Oscillator is most effective in broad trading ranges or slow moving trends. 80 and 20 are the most common levels used but can be adjusted as needed. In general, an area above 80 indicates an overbought region, while the area below 20 is considered an oversold region. A sell signal is given when the oscillator is above the 80 level and then crosses back below 80. Conversely, a buy signal is given when the oscillator is below 20 and then crossed back above 20. When there are two lines are graphed, the slow oscillating %K and a moving average of %K, commonly referred to as %D. When a crossover signal happens as the two lines cross in the overbought or oversold region. A sell signal occurs when a decreasing %K line crosses below the %D line in the overbought region. Conversely, a buy signal occurs when an increasing %K line crosses above the %D line in the oversold region. Divergences form when a new high or low in price is not confirmed by the Stochastic Oscillator. A bullish divergence forms when price make a lower low, but the Stochastic Oscillator forms a higher low. This indicates less downward momentum that could foreshadow a bullish reversal. A bearish divergence forms when price makes a higher high, but the Stochastic Oscillator forms a lower high. This shows less upward momentum that could foreshadow a bearish reversal.


Bollinger Bands

John Bollinger invented an analysis tool called Bollinger Bands in 1983. Bollinger Bands are designed to help traders evaluate price action and volatility. Three different lines are drawn, consists of a middle band (which is a moving average) and an upper and lower band. These upper and lower bands are set above and below the moving average by a certain number of standard deviations of price, thus incorporating volatility. The contraction or narrowing of the bands can foreshadow a significant advance or decline. Once the squeeze play is on, a subsequent band break signals the start of a new move. A new advance starts with a squeeze and subsequent break above the upper band. A new decline starts with a squeeze and subsequent break below the lower band.
